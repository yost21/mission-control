# For Mom — Plain English Guide

Hey — this is the short, no-jargon version to answer your questions.

---

## "Does my checking account / investment account need to be in the will?"

**Short answer:** No, if you already listed the kids as beneficiaries on
those accounts.

**Why:** Any account with a **beneficiary designation** (sometimes called
"payable on death" / POD, or "transfer on death" / TOD) skips the will
entirely and goes straight to the named beneficiary. The bank/brokerage
just needs a death certificate.

**What to do:**

1. Call each bank and brokerage and ask: *"Do I have beneficiaries listed
   on this account?"*
2. If yes → you're set for that account. Ask them to email you a
   confirmation.
3. If no → ask them to send you the beneficiary designation form. Fill in
   the two kids, 50/50, send it back. Takes 10 minutes.

**The will covers everything else** — the house, car, jewelry, cash at
home, random stuff, anything you forgot. The will is your catch-all safety
net.

---

## "Does the will still need to be done then?"

**Yes — 100%.** Here's why:

1. It names an **executor** — the person authorized to deal with banks,
   file taxes, sell the house, etc.
2. It covers **everything without a beneficiary** (your home, car, stuff,
   anything you forgot to designate)
3. It's a **backup** — if a beneficiary dies before you and you forgot to
   update the form, the will catches it
4. It prevents **family confusion / arguments** — clear wishes on paper

Without a will, the state decides everything, and probate is way messier.

## "Can my friend notarize it?"

**If she's officially commissioned as a notary public — yes.** A notary's
job for a will is:

- Check IDs (yours and the witnesses')
- Watch you sign
- Stamp and sign the notary section

She doesn't need to know anything about wills. If she has her commission
and her stamp, she's qualified.

**If she's still in the process or you want a pro:** Google
"mobile notary near me" or "traveling notary." They come to your house.

- Typical cost: **$25–$75** total
- Some will even bring the 2 witnesses with them (ask for a "signing agent"
  or "will signing service")
- Call ahead and say: "I need notarization for a Last Will and Testament,
  and I may need witnesses too"

**Either way works.** The notary is the notary — the document doesn't care
who does it as long as they're commissioned.

---

## "Who should be my witnesses?"

**Anyone EXCEPT:**
- Your kids (they're inheriting — conflict of interest)
- Grandchildren or anyone else inheriting
- Your healthcare agent (for the advance directive)

**Good witness options:**
- Neighbors
- Coworkers
- Friends who aren't named anywhere in the documents
- The mobile notary can often supply them

You need **2 witnesses** for the will. They just watch you sign and then
sign themselves.

---

## "What do I actually need to fill in?"

For the **will** (see `moms-simple-will-DRAFT.md`):

1. Your full legal name
2. Your address (city, county, state)
3. Each kid's full legal name + date of birth
4. What happens if a kid dies before you (Option A = goes to that kid's
   kids / your grandkids — most common choice)
5. Who's your **executor** (usually the more organized of the two kids,
   or whoever lives closer) + a backup
6. State you live in

For the **advance directive** (see `moms-DNR-advance-directive-DRAFT.md`):

1. Your info (name, DOB, address)
2. Who's your **healthcare agent** (usually one of the kids) + backup
3. Check the DNR box if that's your wish
4. Check boxes for which treatments you do/don't want (or "agent decides")
5. Organ donation preference

That's really it. Everything else is standard legal language.

## "What's the day-of plan?"

**Pick a Saturday morning. Do it all at once.**

1. Print both documents (filled in)
2. Have **2 witnesses** at your house (not the kids)
3. Have a **notary** at your house (friend or mobile notary)
4. Sign the will:
   - You sign first, in front of everyone
   - Both witnesses sign, watching each other
   - Notary does the self-proving affidavit page
5. Sign the advance directive (same process)
6. Notary stamps everything
7. Done. Whole thing takes about 30 minutes.

**Total cost:**
- Just the notary: $25–$75
- With an attorney review first (recommended): add $300–$500

---

## "Do I really need a lawyer?"

**Honestly? For your situation — two kids, 50/50 split, straightforward —
you can probably do it without one.** But here's the case for spending the
$300–$500:

- They'll make sure the language matches your state's rules exactly
- They provide the witnesses and notary in one visit
- If anything weird comes up after you're gone, the will is harder to
  challenge
- Peace of mind

**Middle ground:** Fill out the drafts at home (so you know what you want),
then bring them to an attorney for a **1-hour review + signing**. That's
often $200–$400 flat. Way cheaper than having them draft from scratch.

---

## The bottom line

You are not being morbid. You are being **responsible**. The hardest part
is just sitting down to fill in the names. Everything else — notarizing,
witnesses, filing — is easy logistics.

We've got this. Take it one step at a time. ❤️
